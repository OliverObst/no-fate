The fictional North Quay Workshop kept two ledgers. One recorded new
instruments. The other recorded every adjustment, repair, disagreement, and
discarded part. Visitors usually asked to see the first ledger. The technicians
read the second.

## The work after launch

Design is often described through beginnings: the first drawing, the first
prototype, the first public release. Yet most encounters with a system happen
later, when its assumptions meet changing conditions.

Maintenance is not a neutral service applied after the interesting work. It is
where priorities become visible. A repair queue states what matters. A
deprecation notice states who is expected to adapt. A carefully preserved
interface states which forms of trust deserve continuity.

## Records make choices discussable

The workshop's second ledger did not make every decision correct. It made the
decisions available for review. Entries named the observed problem, the change
made, and the next condition to watch.

That modest structure turned maintenance into an editorial practice. It let
later readers distinguish accumulated knowledge from habit.

> A system is designed not only by what it permits today, but by what it makes
> possible to repair tomorrow.

This account, workshop, and quotation are fictional demonstration material.
